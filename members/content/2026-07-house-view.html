In the last full week of June a sharp but narrow sell-off hit the AI complex: the Nasdaq 100 fell 4.2%, semiconductors 7.7% and the Magnificent Seven 5.9%, while the equal-weight S&P rose 1.6%, the Russell 2000 1.0% and healthcare 7.9% — the broadening and defensive rotation we have been positioned for. The Fed, under Warsh, held at 3.50 to 3.75% but turned its dot plot to a 2026 hike and lifted year-end PCE to 3.6%. Risk then stabilised into month-end, with the Nasdaq up 2.1% on 30 June and the S&P closing near 7,440.

We are not changing the architecture of the view, we are leaning further into it. We stay constructive on equities but more selective, tilted to quality, defensives and non-US; we run short duration with a belly and quality-bond hedge; we hold gold through the pullback and would top up; and we lean long the dollar. For the first time, the two adverse regimes together outweigh the continuation path.

1 · Macro and geopolitical outlook

Four regimes, mutually exclusive and summing to 100, with no single dominant base case. This month the balance shifted toward the two adverse regimes, which together carry 55%. The backdrop is hawkish-for-longer policy — the Fed flipping to a 2026 hike, the ECB finishing its hikes with survey inflation expectations easing to 3.5% — and a fragile Middle East truce, with the UK transition from Starmer to Burnham a live fiscal tail.

The Trend column is our read on the direction of travel of each probability: risk is migrating toward the AI air-pocket and, newly, toward the oil shock. Above all four sits the fiscal and bond-market accident — not a regime, but the tail that makes any scenario worse, and the main reason we hold gold and some quality duration.

2 · Key trends, next 12 months

  1. Hawkish-for-longer is now in the dots. The June projection flipped the 2026 median to a hike and raised year-end PCE to 3.6%. Cash and short-duration carry rewarded, long duration vulnerable except in the air-pocket.
  2. The AI capex supercycle meets its first valuation test. Capex above 450 billion dollars with no proportionate return yet identified; the late-June reset was a valuation reset, not a fundamental break. Selectivity over beta.
  3. Equity broadening, confirmed. The sharpest growth-versus-value divergence of the year, with defensives and small caps up. Diversify beyond US mega-cap.
  4. Energy and geopolitics as the swing factor. A fragile ceasefire and energy back in the Fed's reasoning make oil the dominant two-way driver of inflation and risk appetite.
  5. The dollar reasserting. A widening rate gap, the Fed to a hike against an easing ECB, keeps the FX overlay long the dollar.

3 · Structural and long-term trends

  1. AI, power and compute. A multi-year capex and electricity-demand supercycle; own the value chain, not just mega-cap.
  2. Fiscal dominance and high public debt. Higher term premia, periodic bond-market stress, and a tailwind for gold.
  3. Multipolarity and de-globalisation. Reshoring, defence, commodities and a persistent geopolitical risk premium.
  4. Central-bank gold accumulation. Central banks bought 244 tonnes in Q1, above the five-year average, a structural bid under gold.
  5. Institutionalisation of family-office capital. Allocations converging with institutions, especially in private markets at roughly 30%.

4 · Tactical view, one to three months

Constructive but more selective. Risk-on equities tilted to quality, defensives and non-US; short duration with a belly hedge; gold and a long dollar as hedges; modestly more constructive on energy.

Stay long equities but keep broadening into Japan, EM equity, Europe, quality cyclicals, financials, industrials and the AI value chain, and keep trimming US mega-cap. Run short and belly duration for carry, add belly and quality bonds as the air-pocket hedge, hold the 5% gold through the dip and top up, and lift the energy-equity tail hedge on renewed Hormuz risk. Our underweight-duration tilt is a muddle-through and stagflation call; in the air-pocket, that regime is hedged by the belly, quality bonds and gold, not by the short-duration tilt.

5 · Key risks to the thesis

  1. AI capex disappoints on ROI. Spend ahead of monetisation; a retrenchment takes semis, power demand and the earnings base with it. Our biggest structural risk, now part-realised.
  2. Iran and Hormuz re-escalation. A fragile ceasefire; a breakdown reopens the oil and inflation tail.
  3. A fiscal and bond-market accident. A buyers' strike in Treasuries or gilts spikes term premia and resets every discount rate.
  4. Fed credibility under Warsh. A policy error or perceived loss of independence un-anchors inflation expectations and the dollar.
  5. Other geopolitical flashpoints. Taiwan compounds the AI-supply-chain risk; Ukraine and Russia via European energy.
