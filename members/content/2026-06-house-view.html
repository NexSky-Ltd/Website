This is NexSky's first CIO house view. Our editorial line is simple: to give you our own untainted opinion on where we see markets going, transparently and independently, and to provoke conversation and disagreement. We draw on the leading bank and asset-manager outlooks, debate them, and then give you our unfiltered view. The conclusions are ours.

The next twelve months are unusually multi-modal, with the world pulled by opposing forces: inflationary energy, disinflationary AI, and fiscal stress. So rather than a single base case, we frame four regimes of comparable likelihood and position for robustness. We stay constructive on equities while broadening away from concentrated US mega-cap, run short duration, hold gold as a strategic hedge, and lean long the dollar through the overlay.

1 · Macro and geopolitical outlook

Four regimes, mutually exclusive and summing to 100, with no single dominant base case. The backdrop is hawkish-for-longer policy and a fragile truce: the Fed under Warsh on hold through 2026 with the easing bias removed, the ECB finishing its summer hikes, the BoJ hiking gradually, and the SNB at zero. The Middle East de-escalation holds but is fragile, and the UK transition from Starmer to Burnham is a live fiscal tail.

Above all four sits the fiscal and bond-market accident: not a regime of its own, but the tail that makes any of these worse, and the main reason we hold gold and some quality duration rather than optimising the book to one path. The two adverse regimes together carry 45% and break in opposite directions, one deflationary and equity-led, one inflationary and rate-led. That is the case for hedging both ends.

2 · Key trends, next 12 months

  1. Hawkish for longer under Warsh. Cuts are off the table and front-end yields are anchored high. Cash and short-duration carry are rewarded; long duration stays vulnerable.
  2. An AI capex supercycle. Around 2.5% of GDP in build-out, with allocation shifting from buybacks to capex and record equity and bond supply ahead. Selectivity matters and supply weighs on duration.
  3. Energy and geopolitics as the swing factor. Hormuz is binary, and oil is the dominant driver of both inflation and risk appetite.
  4. The dollar reasserting. The DXY broke out above 100.6 as rate differentials swung back to the US, so the FX overlay matters again.
  5. Equity broadening and high dispersion. Mega-cap leadership is wobbling while Japan, the Russell 2000, EM and Europe make new highs. We diversify beyond US mega-cap.

3 · Structural and long-term trends

  1. AI, power and compute. A multi-year capex and electricity-demand supercycle. We favour the value chain across semiconductors, power and infrastructure, not only mega-cap.
  2. Fiscal dominance and high public debt. Structurally higher term premia, periodic bond-market stress with the UK as the canary, and a tailwind for gold.
  3. Multipolarity and de-globalisation. Reshoring, defence, commodities and a persistent geopolitical risk premium.
  4. Central-bank gold accumulation and dollar-reserve diversification. Family offices feel over-exposed to the dollar, giving a structural bid for gold and non-dollar diversifiers.
  5. The institutionalisation of family-office capital. Allocations converging with institutions, especially in private markets at roughly 30% and above.

4 · Tactical view, one to three months

Stance: constructive but selective. Risk-on equities, short duration, hold the hedges. Momentum favours equities over bonds, earnings are strong and volatility is low. Near-term, hawkish-Fed repricing, a firmer dollar and sticky inflation are headwinds for long duration, EM local debt and FX, and gold in the very short run.

What we would do now: stay long equities but broaden into Japan, EM, Europe, quality cyclicals, financials, industrials and the AI value chain, and trim US mega-cap concentration. Run short and belly duration for carry. Hold the strategic 5% gold through any dip. Lean neutral-to-long the dollar through the overlay. Keep a small energy-equity tail hedge on Hormuz optionality.

5 · Key risks to the thesis

  1. The AI capex supercycle disappoints on ROI. Spend is running ahead of monetisation; if AI revenue fails to justify the build-out, capex retrenches and takes semis, power demand and equity leadership with it. Our single biggest structural risk.
  2. Iran and Hormuz re-escalation. A breakdown reopens the oil and inflation tail, forces central banks to tighten harder, and stalls growth.
  3. A fiscal and bond-market accident. A buyers' strike in US Treasuries or UK gilts pushes term premia sharply higher and resets the discount rate under every asset.
  4. Fed credibility under Warsh. A policy error or perceived loss of independence un-anchors inflation expectations and undermines the dollar.
  5. A dollar and reserve shock. A disorderly dollar move that breaks the cross-asset correlations the portfolio relies on.
  6. Other geopolitical flashpoints. Taiwan compounds our risk rather than diversifying it, hitting the AI and semiconductor supply chain directly.
